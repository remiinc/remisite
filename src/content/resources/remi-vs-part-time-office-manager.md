## How much does a part-time office manager really cost?

More than the hourly rate suggests. Office managers average about $23.44 an hour in the US, based on roughly 20,300 reported salaries ([Indeed, updated June 2026](https://www.indeed.com/career/office-manager/salaries)). The Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the median for secretaries and administrative assistants at $47,460 a year for full-time work ([BLS, May 2024](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/office-and-administrative-support/secretaries-and-administrative-assistants.htm)). Those are the sticker prices. The real cost is higher.

On top of wages, an employer pays 7.65% in Social Security and Medicare taxes on every dollar ([IRS Topic 751](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc751)). Then there is workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, any benefits you offer, the software seats they use, and the time you spend hiring, training, and covering the role when they leave. ## What does a part-time office manager do that Remi cannot?

Anything that requires being a person in a room. This is the honest part, and it matters more than the price. A human office manager can do things Remi has no way to do, and if your business leans on these, you need the human.

- Be physically present. Someone to greet a walk-in, sign for a delivery, hand a clipboard to a customer, or hold down a front desk is a body in a building. Remi is not.
- Answer the phone. Remi works over text, email, Slack, and a web portal. Remi is not a phone receptionist. If your day runs on inbound calls and you have not set up a separate answering service, a person answers and Remi does not.
- Handle cash and physical paperwork. Deposits, petty cash, mailing checks, filing signed contracts in a drawer. That is hands-on work.
- Make in-person judgment calls with context you never wrote down. A good office manager reads your mood, knows which customer is a headache, and decides on the spot. Remi works from what is connected and written, not from the room.
- Manage other people and vendors face to face. Scheduling the crew, chasing a supplier in person, smoothing over a tense client meeting. Human work.

If half your list is on that list, hire the person. Remi is not a replacement for a front desk or a phone line, and any tool that tells you otherwise is selling you something.

## How do I decide between a person, Remi, or both?

Start with what the job actually is on a normal week. Write down the tasks the role covers, then sort them. The split is usually clean.

1. List every task you would hand this role. Answering phones, greeting customers, chasing overdue invoices, sending quote follow-ups, filing, deposits, scheduling, reminders.
2. Mark each one "needs a body in the room" or "can be done from what's connected." Phones, front desk, cash, and physical files go in the first pile. Money follow-up, quote chasing, and tracking go in the second.
3. Count the piles. If the "needs a body" pile is most of the job, hire the person. If the "from what's connected" pile is most of it, Remi covers that for about a tenth of the cost.
4. If it is genuinely split, do both. Many owners keep a part-timer for the physical and phone work and let Remi own the money watching, so nothing slips between shifts or after hours.

There is no shame in either answer. A busy shop with a lobby and a ringing phone needs a human at the desk. A remodeler or consultant who works from a truck or a laptop, and whose real leak is unpaid invoices and dead quotes, is paying office-manager money for a problem Remi was built to watch.

## What a two-minute test tells you

Direct answer: you can settle this before you spend a dollar on either. Open your sent folder and your accounting tool and answer three questions.

> How many invoices are more than 30 days out and have had no follow-up?
>
> How many quotes did you send in the last 60 days that never got a yes or a no?
>
> How many customer emails are still sitting unanswered right now?

If those three numbers are near zero, your follow-up is already handled and you may only need a person for the physical work. If they are not near zero, that is the money leaking out, and it is the exact thing a 20-hour-a-week office manager tends to miss between other tasks. It is common: 47% of small businesses report invoices more than 30 days past due ([Intuit QuickBooks, 2025](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/)).
