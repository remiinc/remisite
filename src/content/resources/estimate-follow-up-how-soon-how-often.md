## How soon should you follow up on an estimate?

Follow up within 24 to 48 hours of sending the estimate. That first touch is not a hard sell. It confirms the estimate arrived, invites questions, and keeps you top of mind while the job is still fresh in the customer's head.

Speed matters more than most owners think. Harvard Business Review research on sales leads found that lead quality drops sharply within minutes of an inquiry going cold, and that most companies are far too slow to follow up ([The Short Life of Online Sales Leads, HBR, 2011](https://hbr.org/2011/03/the-short-life-of-online-sales-leads)). The same instinct applies after the quote: the longer the gap, the more your estimate becomes one more thing the customer means to get to and never does.

There is a second reason to move fast. A customer weighing a project is often collecting two or three bids. The business that stays present and responsive reads as the one that will actually show up and do the work. Roughly 78% of customers hire whoever responds first, a finding widely cited in speed-to-lead research and commonly attributed to Lead Connect ([reported by Vendasta](https://www.vendasta.com/blog/lead-response-time/)). Following up quickly is not pestering. It is often the difference between the signed job and the one that ghosts.

## Why do estimates die: price or silence?

Estimates die from silence far more often than from price. When a quote goes nowhere, owners assume they were too expensive. Usually the real story is quieter: the customer got busy, the email slipped down the inbox, they meant to reply and forgot, or they were waiting for a small nudge to feel confident saying yes.

Price objections are actually good news, because they are a conversation. A customer who says the number is high is still talking to you, and scope or timing can often be adjusted. The dangerous outcome is no response at all, because you never learn whether it was budget, timing, trust, or a competitor who simply called them back first.

This is why a follow-up cadence beats a single quote and a hope. Each touch does a job: it surfaces the real objection, keeps you ahead of the competing bid, and gives the customer permission to reply. You are not wearing them down. You are removing the friction between them and a yes.

## How often should you follow up, and for how long?

Follow up three to four times over roughly two weeks, then stop. Any less and you leave money on the table from customers who simply forgot. Any more and you start training people to ignore you. A short, deliberate sequence respects the customer's time and still gives the job every fair chance to close.

Here is a cadence that works for most service estimates. Adjust the timing to your trade: a same-week emergency repair moves faster than a full remodel.

| Touch | Timing | Channel | Goal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Check-in | Day 1 to 2 | Same channel they used | Confirm the estimate arrived, invite questions |
| 2. Value nudge | Day 5 to 7 | Mix in a text if you have the number | Offer a reason to decide now (open slot, options) |
| 3. Close the loop | Day 12 to 14 | Email plus text | Ask for a clear yes, not-now, or no |
| 4. Optional revive | 30 to 45 days | Email | Reopen if their timeline may have changed |

Two rules make the cadence work. First, vary the channel. If email got no reply, a short text often lands where the email didn't. Second, never send an empty "just checking in" with nothing new. Every touch should carry a small reason to respond: an answer, an open slot on your calendar, or a genuine question.

## What should you say in each follow-up?

Say something short, specific, and easy to answer in one line. The best follow-ups reference the exact job, give the customer a concrete next step, and ask one direct question. Here are three templates you can copy, paste, and adjust. Fill the brackets with real details.

### Template 1: The day-2 check-in

Use this within 24 to 48 hours. The tone is helpful, not pushy. You are making sure it landed and opening the door to questions.

> Hi [Name], following up on the estimate I sent [day] for [the job, e.g. the kitchen backsplash]. Wanted to make sure it came through and answer anything before you decide. Happy to walk through any line item. Are you good to move forward, or is there something you'd want adjusted?

### Template 2: The day-7 value nudge

Use this around day 5 to 7 if the check-in got no reply. This one gives the customer a reason to act now and quietly surfaces the real objection.

> Hi [Name], checking in on the [job] estimate from last week. If timing is the question, I have a slot open [week or date] and can hold it for you through [day]. If it's budget, I'm glad to talk through options that still get you what you need. Want me to pencil you in?

### Template 3: The day-14 close-the-loop

Use this around day 12 to 14 as your final touch. It is respectful, it removes pressure, and it makes replying easy by offering a clear choice. This message often gets a response precisely because it signals you are about to stop.

> Hi [Name], I don't want to keep crowding your inbox, so this is my last check-in on the [job] estimate. If you'd still like to move forward, just reply and I'll hold your spot. If the timing isn't right, no problem at all. Should I close this out for now, or keep it open on my end?

The pattern across all three: name the job, give one clear next step, ask one answerable question, and keep it under five sentences. Long, apologetic follow-ups read as unsure. Short and specific reads as a professional who has other work and would be glad to do theirs.

## When should you stop chasing an estimate?

Stop after a clear final message that gets no reply. Once you have sent the day-14 close-the-loop and heard nothing, the honest read is that this job is not happening right now, and continuing to message crosses from persistent into annoying. Mark it closed and move your energy to the next lead.

Stopping is not the same as forgetting. A "no reply now" is often just "not this month." Keep the estimate on file, and set a reminder to send one light revive message in 30 to 45 days, or whenever their season or project window is likely to open. Circumstances change, and a customer who ignored you in a busy stretch may sign the moment their timing lines up. The optional revive touch in the cadence table above is exactly this.

The one time to stop immediately is a clear no. If a customer says they went another direction or the project is off, thank them, leave the door open, and close it out. A gracious exit is worth more than a last pitch. The next time they need work done, you want to be the business they remember as easy to deal with.

## FAQ

### How many times should you follow up on an estimate?

Three to four times over about two weeks is the sweet spot for most service jobs: a check-in around day 2, a value nudge around day 7, and a final close-the-loop around day 14. After a clear last message with no reply, stop and mark it closed. Keep the quote on file for one optional revive touch a month or so later.

### Is it unprofessional to follow up on a quote?

No. A short, specific follow-up reads as organized, not pushy. Most customers who go quiet are busy, not offended, and they often expect you to circle back. The bigger risk is the opposite: silence from you can look like you stopped caring about the job, and the competing bid that keeps showing up looks like the one that wants it.

### Should you follow up on an estimate by text, email, or call?

Match the channel the customer used to reach you first, then vary it. If they emailed, email the check-in, then try a text for the day-7 nudge. Texts tend to get read fastest for service work, but keep a written record either way so you always know what was said and when.

### What do you say when a customer goes silent after a quote?

Give them an easy way to reply and a reason to reply now. Offer a specific open slot on your schedule, ask one direct question they can answer in a word, and make it clear you can adjust the scope if budget is the sticking point. The day-7 value nudge template above is built for exactly this moment.
