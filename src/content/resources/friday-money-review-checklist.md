A weekly cash flow review is a short, fixed check you run every Friday to see who owes you money, what work is still unbilled, and whether you have enough cash for next week. It takes about 15 minutes and covers six things: open invoices by age, quotes waiting on an answer, finished jobs you have not billed, bills coming due, your real cash position, and one follow-up action for each. Run it every Friday and money stops going stale on you.

Most owners check the money once a month, usually when the bookkeeper asks or a bill bounces. That is too slow. Invoices, quotes, and customer patience all decay in days, not quarters. A weekly rhythm catches a silent quote while the customer still remembers the conversation, and a slipping invoice while a friendly nudge still fixes it. This post gives you the exact checklist, a copy-paste version to keep, and the numbers behind why weekly wins. Remi runs this same review automatically, but you do not need her to start. You need 15 minutes and a Friday.

## Why review cash flow weekly instead of monthly?

Because money goes stale in days, not months, and a monthly review finds the damage after it is done. By the time a monthly close shows you a 45-day-old invoice, you have already lost the easy window to collect it politely.

The scale of the problem is not small. QuickBooks' 2025 Small Business Late Payments Report found the average small business is owed about $17,500 in unpaid invoices, and 47% of businesses said some of their invoices were more than 30 days past due ([Intuit QuickBooks, 2025](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/)). In construction and the trades it is worse: roughly 2 out of 3 contractors report being paid late ([Built, 2025](https://getbuilt.com/blog/70-percent-of-contractors-say-payment-delays-threaten-industry/)).

Those numbers do not build up on a monthly clock. An invoice does not jump from fresh to 40 days overdue at month-end. It ages one day at a time, and every week you do not look is a week a collectable invoice drifts toward a hard one. A weekly review is how you catch the drift while it is still cheap to fix.

Here is the difference laid out plainly.

| | Monthly review | Weekly Friday review |
|---|---|---|
| How stale money gets before you see it | Up to 30 days | Up to 7 days |
| Quote follow-up window | Often gone cold | Still warm |
| Unbilled work caught | Weeks later | Same week it finished |
| Cash surprises | Found at month-end | Found with a week to react |
| Time per session | 1 to 2 hours | About 15 minutes |

The weekly version is not more work. It is less work spread thin enough that nothing rots.

## What goes on a weekly cash flow review checklist?

Six things, in order, each with one action so the review ends in decisions instead of a worried feeling. Look at open invoices by age, quotes with no reply, finished-but-unbilled jobs, bills coming due, your cash position, then the single worst item. Do the six in sequence and you cover every place money hides.

The rule that makes it work: each step ends with one concrete follow-up action, not a mental note. A review that only produces awareness is a review you will dread. A review that produces six small actions is one you will keep.

## The 15-minute Friday money review, step by step

Set a 15-minute timer and go in this order. The times are a guide, not a race.

1. Open invoices by age (2 minutes). Pull your unpaid invoices and sort oldest first. You are looking for anything past its due date, especially the 30-day-plus bucket. Action: draft a reminder for the single oldest overdue invoice and send it today.

2. Quotes awaiting an answer (2 minutes). Scan your sent quotes and estimates for ones with no reply. A quote with silence on it is not a maybe, it is a follow-up you have not made yet. Speed matters here: in widely cited lead-response research, most customers hire whoever responds first ([lead response study summary](https://caseyresponse.com/blog/lead-response-time-statistics)). Action: send one short nudge on the oldest silent quote.

3. Jobs done but not billed (3 minutes). List work you have finished or delivered this week that has no invoice yet. This is the most common leak, and it is entirely your own money sitting on your side of the fence. Action: create and send at least one invoice for completed work right now.

4. Bills coming due (2 minutes). Look at what you owe over the next 7 to 14 days: suppliers, subs, software, rent, payroll. You are checking that money going out lines up with money coming in. Action: flag any bill due before your next expected deposit.

5. Cash position (2 minutes). Take your real bank balance and subtract the bills from step 4. That gap is the only cash number that matters this week. Do not trust the top-line balance, trust the balance minus what is already spoken for. Action: if the gap is thin, decide today which incoming payment you most need to chase.

6. The one thing to fix (4 minutes). Look back at everything you flagged and pick the single most at-risk item. The oldest invoice, the biggest silent quote, the bill you cannot cover. Action: take one concrete step on it now, a call, a text, a firmer reminder, or a payment plan offer.

That is the whole review. Six looks, six actions, roughly 15 minutes. The finished-but-unbilled step alone usually pays for the time.

## A copy-paste Friday money review checklist

Keep this somewhere you will see it on Fridays. It is written to be used verbatim.

> Friday Money Review (15 min)
>
> 1. Open invoices, oldest first. Anything 30+ days? Send a reminder on the oldest one.
> 2. Quotes with no reply. Nudge the oldest silent one.
> 3. Finished work not yet invoiced. Send at least one invoice now.
> 4. Bills due in the next 1 to 2 weeks. Flag anything due before my next deposit.
> 5. Bank balance minus those bills = real cash. Is it thin?
> 6. Pick the single worst item. Do one thing about it before I close the laptop.
>
> Done. Next Friday, same list.

If you want a running log instead of a throwaway note, add three columns to a page or spreadsheet: date, the item you acted on, and the follow-up owed. Over a month you will see your own patterns, like which customers always need two nudges.

## How do I make the weekly review actually stick?

Attach it to something that already happens on Fridays so you never have to remember it, and keep the scope small enough that a tired brain can finish it.

A few things that help:

- Same time every week. End of day Friday, right before you stop, works because the week's jobs are done and reminders land before the weekend.
- One tool, one balance, one sent folder. You do not need a dashboard. Your invoicing tool, your bank login, and your email sent items cover all six steps.
- Actions over analysis. If a step tempts you into a 20-minute rabbit hole, note it and move on. The review's job is to surface and nudge, not to solve everything.
- Protect the 15 minutes. It is the cheapest hour you spend all month, just cut into quarters.

This is general operating advice, not accounting or legal advice. If a specific unpaid invoice heads toward late fees, liens, or small claims, rules vary by state and you should check a real authority like your [state's small claims court information](https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer) or an accountant before acting.

## FAQ

### How long does a weekly money review actually take?

About 15 minutes once you know the six things to look at. The first one or two take longer while you find where each number lives. After that it settles into a quarter of an hour every Friday.

### What day should I do my money review?

Friday works for most owners because the week's work is billed and you can send follow-ups before the weekend. Any fixed day beats no day. The point is that it repeats on a schedule you do not have to think about.

### Do I need accounting software to do this?

No. You can run the whole checklist from your invoicing tool, your email sent folder, and your bank balance. Software like QuickBooks makes the aging step faster, but the review works with a notebook and a bank login.

### Is a weekly review replacing my monthly bookkeeping?

No. Bookkeeping reconciles the past for taxes and clean records. The Friday review is forward-looking and about what to chase this week. You need both, and they do different jobs.

### What if I find money I forgot to bill?

That is the review working. Unbilled finished work is the most common leak owners find. Invoice it the same day you spot it, because the clock on getting paid does not start until the invoice goes out.
