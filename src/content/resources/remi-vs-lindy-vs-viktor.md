Short answer: pick the tool that matches how you actually work. Remi is for the owner of a small service business who does the job and chases the money, watches your inbox and books, and speaks up first when an invoice goes stale or a quote gets no reply. [Lindy](https://www.lindy.ai/) is a personal AI work assistant and no-code agent builder for busy professionals living in email, meetings, and CRM. [Viktor](https://viktor.com/) is a technical AI coworker that lives in Slack and Microsoft Teams and executes end-to-end work for teams, including building and deploying software.

All three are genuinely good at what they are built for. The differences that matter are who each one is for, how you set it up, what it watches on its own, and whether it acts without asking. This post lays those out with verified pricing and a side-by-side table, then tells you honestly which reader should pick which.

If you run a service business and the thing keeping you up is money you are owed, Remi is the closest fit. If you want a general assistant or a team automation platform, Lindy or Viktor may serve you better. Here is the detail.

## Which one should you pick?

Pick Remi if you run an owner-led service business of 1 to 15 people and the pain is unpaid invoices, quotes dying in silence, and nothing written down. Remi connects to the tools you already run, watches them, and flags what is slipping before you have to go looking.

Pick Lindy if you are a busy professional who wants your inbox, calendar, and meeting follow-ups handled across connected apps, and you are comfortable configuring agents to do it.

Pick Viktor if you have a team that wants a technical coworker inside Slack or Teams that can pull data, build dashboards, write code, and ship internal tools on its own compute.

None of these is a bad tool. They are aimed at different people. The rest of this post shows where each one wins.

## Who is each one actually built for?

Remi is built for the owner who is the business. Remodelers, contractors, home services, agencies, consultancies, trades, legal. Companies of roughly 1 to 15 people where the same person quotes the job, does the job, and then has to chase the check. Remi's whole design assumes you are tired, skeptical, and owed money.

Lindy positions itself as a personal AI work assistant for inbox, meetings, calendar, scheduling, follow-ups, and CRM updates across connected apps, per its own site. It is strongest when one person wants the whole work loop handled and does not mind setting up agents to get there. It is also a no-code agent builder, so power users can wire up triggers like "new email" or "CRM update" and define what happens next.

Viktor calls itself "the AI employee that lives in Slack and Microsoft Teams," per its homepage. It executes work end-to-end, has its own compute environment, and can pull from thousands of tools, generate reports, process invoices, write code, and deploy apps. It raised a $75 million Series A led by Accel in 2026, [as reported by Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/05/19/viktor-ai-startup-raises-75-million-for-virtual-coworker-exclusive/). It is aimed at founders, marketing, engineering, and operations teams.

The plain read: Remi is for one owner and their money. Lindy is for one professional and their inbox. Viktor is for a team and its operational and technical work.

## What does each one watch, and when does it speak first?

This is the biggest real difference, and it is easy to miss. Remi is designed to speak up first. You connect your email and books, and she watches them and surfaces the invoice going stale, the quote that got no reply, and the customer waiting on an answer, without you asking. You do not have to remember to check.

Lindy watches what you tell it to watch. Its agent builder is trigger-based: you define the event that kicks a workflow off, like a new email or a calendar change, and it runs from there. That is powerful and flexible, and it means the watching is only as good as the agents you build.

Viktor works from tasks you assign inside Slack or Teams. You mention it with a request and it executes, drawing on its integrations and compute. It is built to do a lot on command. Standing, unprompted watching of your money is not its headline job.

If the outcome you want is "tell me what I am missing before it costs me," Remi's speak-first design is the closest match. If you want an assistant or coworker that acts on request, Lindy and Viktor are strong.

## How does each one handle approvals and receipts?

Remi's rule is fixed and narrow on purpose. She prepares, drafts, proposes, flags, tracks, and reminds. Things get sent, or marked paid or done, only with your approval, and every action carries a receipt. Everything is on the record: receipts, sources, and an exportable history. If you fire Remi, you keep everything.

Lindy offers approval controls before communications go out, per its site, so you can require a human review before a message sends. That is a real and useful guardrail, and you configure it as part of your setup.

Viktor is built to execute end-to-end, which is its selling point, and you scope how much latitude it has. For teams that want speed and trust the tool with a defined lane, that autonomy is the whole appeal.

The honest framing: Remi trades some autonomy for a hard guarantee that nothing about your money or your customers happens without your say-so and a paper trail. Viktor leans toward autonomy, and Lindy sits in between with configurable approvals.

## Where Lindy is the better pick

Lindy is the better pick if your day is email, meetings, and calendar, and you want one assistant to handle the whole loop across connected apps. Its no-code builder with triggers and conditional logic is flexible and well regarded. Solo professionals, sales reps, recruiters, and customer success folks who live in their inbox will get real value, and the 7-day trial needs no card.

## Where Viktor is the better pick

Viktor is the better pick if you have a team and want a coworker that does technical and operational work on its own. Pulling data from thousands of tools, building dashboards, writing code, and deploying internal apps are things Viktor is genuinely built to do, from inside Slack or Teams where teams already talk. If your bottleneck is analytical or engineering work rather than getting paid, Viktor is aimed squarely at you.

## Where Remi is the better pick, and the honest close

Remi is the better pick when you are the owner, the pain is money, and you want someone watching without being asked. The problem is real and measured: the 2025 [Intuit QuickBooks Small Business Late Payments Report](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/) found small businesses owed money averaged about $17,500 in unpaid invoices, and 47% reported invoices more than 30 days past due. On the front end, responding to a lead within five minutes makes you roughly 21x more likely to qualify it than waiting thirty, [per the MIT Lead Response Management study](https://www.leadresponsemanagement.org/lrm_study). Every stale invoice and silent quote is money on the table.

You can run all of this yourself. Set a weekly calendar block to age your receivables, draft your own reminder templates, and check every quote for a reply after 48 hours. That manual system works, and it costs nothing. The catch is that you have to be the one doing it, usually at 9pm after the kids are down.

If you would rather not be that person, this is the exact job Remi was built for. She watches your inbox and your books, flags what is going stale, and drafts the follow-up for your approval. Nothing goes out, and nothing gets marked paid, without your say-so, and every action comes with a receipt. Remi is an AI teammate. We say so because she will never pretend otherwise, and neither will we.
