## The field-to-office handoff

A construction team was coordinating active jobs across site texts, Slack threads, email, weekly client calls, and a project tracker. The right people were involved, but the handoffs were easy to miss.

A superintendent would flag a client ask from the site. An office lead would mention a change in a meeting. A vendor date would shift in email. Each signal made sense on its own, but the full picture depended on someone remembering where it happened.

## Signals Remi kept attached

Remi watched the surfaces where jobsite work was already moving and kept the source attached to the next step.

- Site and office threads with client asks, timing changes, missing materials, and access needs
- Email replies that needed a client-safe draft or an internal handoff
- Meeting notes with promised follow-ups and inspection timing
- Project tracker references for owner, due date, and current status

The team still reviewed the work. Remi prepared the packet that made review faster.

## The packet Remi prepared

For each open loop, Remi assembled the client ask, the likely owner, the relevant source, and a proposed next step. When a reply was useful, Remi drafted it for review. When the tracker looked stale, Remi proposed the change instead of treating it as completed.

That distinction mattered. Construction work moves quickly, but client-visible updates still need judgment.

## Why it helped

The team could see the jobsite asks that were waiting, which source created them, and who needed to make the next call. Less work had to live in someone’s memory after the site day ended.

The practical win was shorter handoff lag: fewer follow-ups disappeared between the field, the office, and the tracker before the next client conversation.

## The morning coordination habit

The team began reviewing Remi-prepared follow-up packets during its morning coordination rhythm. The packet gave the team a source-backed way to decide what should be sent, assigned, or clarified before the client had to ask again.
