## The after-call follow-up problem

A home services team was balancing new calls, technician notes, estimates, approvals, invoices, and client replies. The work was not messy because the team lacked a process. It was messy because the process kept moving while everyone was already on the next job.

An estimate needed a nudge. A client approved one part of the work but asked a question about another. A technician left a note that changed the next appointment. The signals were there, just scattered.

## Signals from the working day

Remi watched the day-to-day surfaces where service work moved from request to quote to approval.

- Intake notes and client messages with new details
- Technician notes that changed scope, timing, or materials
- Estimate threads waiting on a client response
- Calendar context for promised callbacks and appointment windows

## The list Remi prepared

Remi surfaced estimate follow-ups and flagged approval checks that needed review. When a client reply was useful, Remi drafted it with the relevant job details attached.

The team stayed in control of what went out. Remi made the next useful move easier to see before the day filled up again.

## Why it helped

The team got a clearer view of which clients were waiting, what they were waiting on, and whether the next step was a reply, an approval check, or an internal handoff.

The business outcome was a tighter response window on estimates, so fewer client promises had to sit in someone's head between appointments.

## The afternoon review

The team began using Remi-prepared follow-up lists during afternoon review. The list helped them decide which estimates needed a draft reply, which approvals needed clarification, and which appointments needed updated context before the next visit.
