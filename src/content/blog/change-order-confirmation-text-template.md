A change order confirmation text is a short message you send before any extra work starts. It states one thing that changed, the price difference, the effect on the schedule, and a single line asking the client to reply YES to approve. You send it, you wait for the reply, and only then do you start. The reply is a timestamped, written record of what both of you agreed to. Remi can draft that text for you the moment a job scope moves, but the template below works whether you use her or type it yourself.

Here is the master template. Copy it, keep it in your phone, and change the details per job.

> Quick change on [job/address]. To [do the extra work], it adds [$X] and about [Y days] to the finish. New total is [$Z]. Reply YES to approve and I will get on it. No rush, but I do not want to start until you have signed off.

That is the whole thing. Four facts and a yes. The reason this beats a handshake in the driveway is simple: two out of three contractors are paid late, according to [Built's 2025 research on construction payment delays](https://getbuilt.com/blog/70-percent-of-contractors-say-payment-delays-threaten-industry/), and a large share of that unpaid work is change orders nobody wrote down. The text is not paperwork for its own sake. It is the difference between getting paid for the extra and eating it.

## What should a change order confirmation text include?

A good change order text answers four questions before the client has to ask them. Scope, money, time, and approval. Miss any one and you have left a crack for a dispute to grow in.

1. Scope. Name the exact change in plain words. Not "the bathroom stuff." Say "moving the vanity plumbing to the north wall."
2. Price delta. State the added cost as its own number, then the new total. People argue about totals when they never saw the increment.
3. Schedule impact. Say how many days it adds, even if the answer is zero. Silence on timing is how a two-day add becomes "you said it would be done Friday."
4. Approval line. Ask for a specific reply. "Reply YES to approve." A clear ask gets a clear answer you can screenshot.

Keep it short enough to read on a phone in one glance. If it needs scrolling, it needs to be a signed change order document instead, which we cover below.

## What does the text look like for a small change?

For a small change, keep it to two sentences and a price. Small changes are where most money leaks, because they feel too minor to bother writing up, so they get done on trust and billed on hope.

> On [address]: adding [the extra outlet in the hallway] runs [$180] and does not change the finish date. Reply YES and it is done today.

Send it, get the YES, do the work. The whole exchange takes under a minute and it converts a favor into a line item you can invoice without a conversation.

## What does the text look like for a big change that needs a signed document?

For a big change, the text is the heads-up, not the approval. You still send a message so the client is not blindsided, but the approval itself lives in a signed change order document.

> [Address]: the framing change we talked about is a bigger one. It adds [$4,200] and about [5 days] to the schedule, new total [$X]. I am sending a change order to sign so we both have it in writing. Once it is signed I will order materials and start. Want me to email or text it over?

Written change order requirements are not just good practice. In some states they are the law for residential work. California, for example, requires that home improvement change orders be [in writing and signed by both parties before the work begins](https://www.cslb.ca.gov/Consumers/Hire_A_Contractor/Home_Improvement_Contracts/What_Is_A_Contract.aspx), per the Contractors State License Board. Rules vary by state, and this is general information, not legal advice, so check your own contractor licensing board for what your jobs require. When in doubt on a large or residential change, get the signature.

## What does the text look like when the client asked for the extra?

When the client requested the extra work themselves, confirm it anyway, in writing, with the price attached. A client-requested extra feels like it does not need approval because they asked for it. That is exactly the one that gets done and never billed, because in everyone's memory it was "just a small thing they wanted."

> Happy to [add the second coat in the den] like you asked. That is [$320] more and no change to the timeline, new total [$X]. Reply YES so I have it on the record and I will take care of it.

You are not asking permission to do work they requested. You are putting a number on it before you start, so the invoice at the end is a formality instead of a surprise.

## Why does a text beat a verbal okay every time?

A text beats a verbal okay because a text still exists when the invoice is disputed. A verbal agreement is a memory contest, and the person who owes money tends to remember the smaller number.

Here is the same change handled three ways, and what you are left holding when the client pushes back at billing time.

| Method | Written record | Timestamp | Holds up in a dispute |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verbal okay on site | None | None | Your word against theirs |
| Confirmation text with YES | The thread | Yes | Strong for small and mid changes |
| Signed change order document | The signed doc | Yes | Strongest, and required in some states |

The confirmation text sits in the sweet spot for the everyday change. It is fast enough that you will actually do it, and it produces a record the same way the signed document does. The QuickBooks 2025 Small Business Late Payments Report found the average small business is owed [$17,500 in unpaid invoices, with 47% of those invoices already 30 or more days past due](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/). Undocumented change orders are a quiet feeder into that number. Every one you confirm in writing is one that cannot turn into a "that was included, right?" three weeks later.

## The one habit that makes all of this work

No work starts until the reply lands. That is the entire discipline. The template is easy. The habit is the hard part, because the client is standing right there and the crew is ready and it feels slow to wait for a text back.

Wait anyway. The moment you start extra work before the YES, you have handed the client every reason to argue the price later, and you have taught them that your prices are negotiable after the fact. When the reply comes in, you have a green light you can point to. When it does not come in, you have just found out the client was not actually sold on the change, and you found out before you spent your money on it instead of after.

Build the habit in three steps.

1. Draft the text before you touch the tools. Scope, price delta, schedule, approval line.
2. Send it and set the tool down. Do the next thing on the original scope while you wait.
3. Start the extra only when YES is in the thread. Screenshot or leave it in place for the invoice.

That is the whole system. Four facts, one yes, and the patience to wait for it.

## FAQ

### Is a text message enough to approve a change order?

For small changes on most jobs, a text where the client replies YES to a clear scope and price creates a timestamped written record, which is far stronger than a verbal okay. Larger changes and residential work in some states need a signed change order document. Rules vary by state, so check your contractor licensing board.

### What should a change order confirmation text include?

Four things: what is changing, the price difference, how it affects the schedule, and a clear line asking the client to reply YES to approve. Keep it short enough to read on a phone.

### When do I need a signed change order instead of a text?

When the dollar amount is large, when your contract or state law requires signed written changes, or when the change alters the completion date or major materials. In California, for example, home improvement change orders must be in writing and signed before the work starts.

### What if the client asked for the extra work themselves?

Still send the text. A client-requested extra is the easiest change to confirm and the easiest to forget to bill. Put the price on it in writing before you pick up a tool.

### Why not just handle it verbally to keep the job moving?

Because a verbal okay is not something you can show later. When the invoice is disputed, the text thread is your proof of what was agreed and when. A verbal agreement is a memory contest.
