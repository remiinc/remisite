# Homepage Copy Options

Date: 2026-07-09
Status: raw material for Hunter's editorial pass. Five options per chapter against `docs/homepage-story-map.md`. Each option is annotated with the move it makes so you can mix and rebuild. Nothing here is final copy; treat every line as a first draft to beat.

Craft sources mimicked: Lassie's identity-affirmation and show-the-work plainness, Viktor's hire framing and honesty posture, Rosie's price-and-friction bluntness, Bond's salary anchor (adapted), and the current live door at helloremi.ai (which already runs the hire frame: "The last operations hire you'll ever need", aimed at the old buyer).

Register rules enforced throughout: money before minutes, no "AI" in headlines, no "assistant", claim-discipline verbs, no em dashes, every line written to survive the contractors' Facebook group.

## Chapter 0: Hero subhead

Sits under the fixed H1: "Your business should run. It shouldn't run you."

1. **The division of labor (recommended starting point)**
   "Remi is a teammate you hire over text. She chases the money, follows up on quotes, and keeps every agreement on the record. You run the work. She runs the business side."
   *Move: the job split in owner words; last two sentences are the whole positioning.*

2. **The self-qualifier**
   "For owners who still do the invoicing, the chasing, and the remembering themselves. Remi takes the business side. You keep the trade."
   *Move: the first sentence qualifies the reader the way the ad doctrine demands; a founder bounces, Mike leans in.*

3. **The money lead**
   "Remi gets you paid. She watches your inbox, chases what you're owed, nudges quotes before they go cold, and writes down what everyone agreed to. Nothing goes out without your yes."
   *Move: pain #1 first, approval promise last; the most conversion-shaped of the five.*

4. **The hire you've been putting off**
   "The hire you've been putting off. Remi runs the business side: invoices, follow-ups, and what got agreed to, all from your texts."
   *Move: Viktor's "not a tool, a hire" energy pointed at a man who actually hires; shortest option.*

5. **The plain job list**
   "Remi is a new hire for the business side. She chases invoices, follows up on quiet quotes, remembers what you told every customer, and texts you before you had to ask."
   *Move: Rosie plainness; ends on "she texts first" as the differentiator.*

CTA pair options (primary / secondary):
- "Text Remi" / "See what's slipping"
- "See what's slipping" / "Watch her work" (anchor scroll to Chapter 2)
- "Let Remi take a look" / "Text Remi"

## Chapter 1: Recognition line

Replaces "Remi takes care of the work that follows you home." The "Meet Remi" kicker stays above it. Test: a SaaS founder should not think "that's me too."

1. "Somebody owes you money right now. Remi is already drafting the reminder."
   *Move: present tense, second person, pain #1; the reader supplies the name himself, which is the recognition.*

2. "Chasing invoices. Nudging quotes. Remembering what you told the client in April. That's Remi's job now."
   *Move: three named scenes then the hire punchline; "that's her job now" is the relief line.*

3. "It's 9pm and you're still invoicing. Remi was about to text you about that."
   *Move: the kitchen-table scene plus "she speaks first" smuggled in early; the wittiest option, check it against the serious-money register.*

4. "The quote went quiet. The invoice went unpaid. Nobody wrote down what got agreed. Remi catches all three."
   *Move: pains 1, 2, 3 in ratified order, one clause each; the most disciplined option.*

5. "Remi handles the part of the business that keeps you up: who owes you, who's waiting on you, and what you promised."
   *Move: mood line rebuilt with the three concrete objects it was missing; closest evolution of the current line.*

## Chapter 2: iPhone section framing

Job: make "she texted first" impossible to miss. Header plus one support line.

1. "You didn't text her first. She texted you."
2. "Notice who starts the conversation."
3. "You don't check in on Remi. She checks in on you."
4. "Nobody asked her to look. That's the job."
5. "The first text is hers. It usually is."

Support line (works under any of the five):
"Remi watches the sources you connect and speaks up when something needs you: an invoice ages, a quote goes quiet, a customer is left waiting. You approve what happens next."

*Note: option 1 is the bluntest statement of the only claim no competitor can make. Option 4 ties it to the hire frame.*

## Chapter 3: How it works (first-week arc)

Five sets of three tile headlines, sequenced as time. Bodies are yours; each set's third beat must land on "she speaks up."

**Set A**
"Day one: give her the inbox." / "Week one: she learns how you run things." / "Then she starts speaking up."
*Move: literal new-hire calendar; the most explicit onboarding framing.*

**Set B**
"Start with your inbox." / "She learns your customers, your jobs, your money." / "Then the texts start coming to you."
*Move: the second tile names the three things he cares about; the third flips the direction of the product.*

**Set C**
"Connect Gmail. That's the setup." / "She pieces the business together." / "Then she texts first."
*Move: Rosie-style friction kill in tile one; five words of setup promise.*

**Set D**
"She starts where the money is." / "She learns who owes what, and who's waiting." / "She speaks up before you have to remember."
*Move: money-first throughout; the third line is the memory promise and the initiative promise in one.*

**Set E**
"Give her the inbox." / "Watch her learn the business." / "Then let her talk."
*Move: shortest set, imperative voice, reads like instructions to a new boss; the most confident register.*

## Chapter 6: The close

### Capstone headline (the after-picture)

1. "Run your business from your phone. Actually."
   *Move: claims the phrase every tool has cheapened, then the one-word differentiator.*
2. "The whole business, from one text thread."
3. "The office is your text thread now."
4. "The business runs. Your evenings come back."
   *Move: the only place on the page where the time pain is allowed to cash out, as the after-picture.*
5. "Everything you just read happens in your texts."
   *Move: self-aware close that ties the story together; most conversational.*

### The straight-talk block (fallibility + reversibility)

1. "Remi is good. She is not perfect. That's why nothing reaches a customer without your yes, and every action she takes has a receipt you can check."
2. "New hires make mistakes. Remi asks before anything goes out, keeps a record of everything she does, and shows her sources when you ask."
   *Move: the hire frame absorbs the fallibility concession; a new hire making mistakes is normal, not scary.*
3. "She'll get one wrong now and then. That's why she drafts and you approve. Every send waits for you. Every action goes on the record."
4. "We built her to ask, not guess. Anything customer-facing waits for your approval. Anything she claims, she can show you where it came from."
5. **Reversibility rider (pairs with any of the above):**
   "And if you fire her? You keep everything. Every record, every receipt, every customer note. Yours to export, nothing held hostage."

AI disclosure (canon, verbatim, placed at the end of this block):
"Remi is an AI teammate. We say so because she'll never pretend otherwise, and neither will we."

### Price line

1. "Pro is $99 a month. Scale is $199. No credits, no seats, no math."
2. "$99 a month. Compare that to the office manager you keep not hiring."
   *Move: the adapted Bond anchor; flagged as an open decision in the story map, test before committing.*
3. "One price, everything she does: $99 a month. Need more capacity? Scale is $199."

### The audit-frame offer (headline + body)

1. "See what's slipping through your inbox."
   "Connect Gmail and Remi runs a free inspection: unpaid invoices, quotes gone quiet, promises nobody wrote down. You get the report in about a minute. If you don't hire her, we delete everything."
   *Move: the ratified frame stated straight; the deletion promise as the offer's last word.*

2. "The free estimate, but for your business."
   "She looks, she reports, you decide. Keep the report either way. We delete the rest unless you bring her on."
   *Move: names the cultural analogy outright; he has closed a thousand deals with this play from the other side.*

3. "Let her take a look first."
   "Sixty seconds after you connect your inbox, Remi shows you what's slipping: money owed, threads dropped, quotes dying. No hire, nothing kept."

4. "Find out what's sitting in your inbox."
   "Most owners are owed more than they think. Remi shows you exactly what and where. You keep the report; we delete everything else unless you hire her."
   *Move: curiosity plus the undersell-by-design posture; "exactly what and where" is the receipt promise.*

5. "Before you pay a dime, make her prove it."
   "Connect the inbox. She shows you what's slipping. Don't like what you see? We delete it all and part friends."
   *Move: hands the skeptic the power position; "make her prove it" is his own voice.*

CTA button options: "See what's slipping" (recommended) / "Show me what's slipping" / "Run the free inspection" / "Let Remi take a look" / "Text Remi".
