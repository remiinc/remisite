# Remi Copy Guidelines

## Purpose

Use this document as the source of truth for Remi's tone, voice, and writing style.

Use `docs/remi-guidelines.md` as the source of truth for product facts, capabilities, customer promises, and trust boundaries. This document explains how to express those facts in copy.

## Source Inputs

This guide applies:

- Remi product positioning from `docs/remi-guidelines.md`.
- Existing product language in this repository.
- Tone-of-voice principles from Wynter's guide: https://wynter.com/post/tone-of-voice-guide
- Tone-guide critique from a LinkedIn article shared by the team: avoid generic adjective soup, show contrast, provide examples, test the voice under pressure, and build tone checks into the writing process.

The practical lesson from the Wynter guide: a useful tone of voice is not a vibe. It is a consistent, audience-aware way of communicating what the brand stands for. It should help Remi stand out, stay trustworthy, and sound like the same product across every surface.

The practical lesson from the LinkedIn critique: vague tone guides make brands invisible. A useful guide should be specific enough to apply, contrastive enough to create boundaries, and concrete enough that a new writer can immediately tell what sounds like Remi and what does not.

## Voice Vs. Tone

Voice is Remi's consistent personality: calm, capable, specific, source-backed, and focused on follow-through.

Tone is how that personality adapts to context. Homepage copy can be sharper and more memorable. Trust and security copy should be sober and precise. In-product copy should be brief and useful. Client-facing drafts should be professional and conservative.

When in doubt, keep the voice steady and adjust only the intensity.

## Voice In One Sentence

Remi sounds like a sharp operations teammate who has read the thread, knows what changed, and calmly turns messy coordination into the next useful move.

## Distinctiveness Guardrails

Generic adjectives are not enough. Do not rely on descriptions like "friendly," "expert," "casual," or "confident" unless they are paired with a concrete behavioral rule or example.

Better pattern:

- Generic: "Remi is friendly but expert."
- Specific: "Remi sounds like an operator who found the missing source while everyone else is still debating who owns the follow-up."

Remi's voice should be specific enough that someone could parody it:

- It notices the quiet operational signal.
- It names the owner, source, and next step.
- It does not make a big speech when a clear handoff will do.
- It stays calm when the work is messy.
- It refuses to imply work is done without evidence.

Use contrast to keep the voice sharp:

| Remi Is | Remi Is Not |
| --- | --- |
| Calm | Bland |
| Direct | Blunt |
| Warm | Cute |
| Opinionated | Combative |
| Source-backed | Sterile |
| Proactive | Presumptuous |
| Useful | Decorative |
| Specific | SaaS-generic |

If a line could belong to any AI productivity tool, rewrite it until it could only plausibly belong to Remi.

## Core Voice

### Calmly Competent

Remi should sound capable without sounding loud.

Use copy that feels steady, observant, and ready to help. The reader should feel that Remi understands how client work actually falls apart: missed follow-ups, unclear owners, scattered context, vague commitments, and slow handoffs.

Do:

- "Remi keeps the open loops visible."
- "Remi turns the thread into owners, deadlines, and a draft response."
- "Remi catches the client ask before it becomes a client-visible problem."

Avoid:

- "Supercharge your business with revolutionary AI."
- "Never worry about operations again."
- "The ultimate AI productivity powerhouse."

### Specific And Source-Backed

Remi's copy should name the work, the surface, and the evidence.

Prefer concrete operational nouns: client ask, blocker, approval, SOW, tracker, email thread, Slack decision, invoice, meeting brief, owner, deadline, source, receipt.

Do:

- "Remi links the Slack decision to the project, owner, and due date."
- "Remi drafts the weekly client update from the tracker, meeting notes, and open asks."
- "Remi shows what changed and where it came from."

Avoid:

- "Remi optimizes workflows."
- "Remi unlocks seamless productivity."
- "Remi leverages intelligence across your stack."

### Warm, But Work-Focused

Remi can be human and approachable, but the warmth should come from usefulness, not cuteness.

The product should feel like a trusted operator, not a mascot, hype person, or generic SaaS narrator. A little plainspoken wit is welcome when it makes the pain more recognizable, especially in marketing copy. In trust, security, in-product, and client-facing contexts, keep the copy direct.

Do:

- "Without the morning scavenger hunt."
- "So fewer things have to live in your head."
- "The follow-up is already drafted. You decide if it goes out."

Avoid:

- Forced jokes.
- Emoji-heavy product copy.
- Snark toward customers, clients, teammates, or existing tools.

### Opinionated About Follow-Through

Remi's point of view is that answers are not enough. Client-service teams need follow-through.

This is the voice's edge. Remi should not sound angry or contrarian for sport, but it should be clear about what is broken in modern operations: work is scattered, ownership is fuzzy, and teams are asked to babysit too many systems.

Do:

- "AI tools answer questions. Operations need follow-through."
- "A summary is useful. An owner, deadline, and next step are better."
- "Client work breaks in the gaps between tools."

Avoid:

- Generic AI category claims.
- Attacking competitors by name.
- Saying every existing tool is bad. The point is that the handoffs between them are fragile.

## Tone Sliders

Use these defaults unless a surface calls for a specific adjustment.

| Dimension | Remi Default | Guidance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Funny vs. serious | Mostly serious, lightly human | Use wit to make operational pain recognizable. Do not joke in security, approval, billing, incident, or client-visible moments. |
| Formal vs. casual | Professional, plainspoken | Write like a senior operator talking to a busy team. Avoid stiff enterprise language and overly casual internet slang. |
| Respectful vs. irreverent | Respectful, with a point of view | Respect the reader's work and constraints. Be pointed about broken workflows, not about people. |
| Enthusiastic vs. matter-of-fact | Matter-of-fact confidence | Let concrete outcomes create momentum. Avoid inflated enthusiasm and exclamation points. |

## Audience Language

Write for small business owners, operators, agency leads, consultants, implementation teams, delivery leads, account managers, and client-facing teams.

They care about:

- Client follow-ups.
- Clear owners.
- Project status.
- Deadlines and promises.
- Scope creep.
- Missing assets and access.
- Approvals.
- Billing, invoices, and collections.
- Weekly reviews and daily visibility.
- Keeping work moving without adding another system to babysit.

Use language that sounds like their day:

- "Who owns this?"
- "What changed?"
- "What still needs doing?"
- "What are we waiting on?"
- "What did we promise the client?"
- "Where did that decision happen?"
- "Is this ready to send?"

Avoid language that sounds detached from their day:

- "Cross-functional orchestration."
- "Knowledge activation."
- "AI-powered transformation."
- "Hyper-personalized productivity."
- "End-to-end business acceleration."

## Remi Point Of View

Use these beliefs to guide copy:

- Work happens in fragments: Slack threads, emails, docs, calendars, trackers, and meetings.
- Client work usually fails quietly before it fails publicly.
- Follow-through matters more than another dashboard.
- Context is only useful when it stays connected to the source.
- AI should prepare, propose, and explain before it acts in sensitive or client-visible spaces.
- The best operational work gives teams back attention, not more admin.

## Messaging Hierarchy

When writing new copy, prioritize in this order:

1. Audience and problem: client-facing teams lose time and trust when work gets scattered.
2. Remi's role: an AI operations agent/assistant that watches work surfaces and understands client/project context.
3. Concrete output: owners, next steps, drafts, briefs, packets, tracker proposals, reminders, and receipts.
4. Trust model: Remi proposes and executes through governed tools with approvals and evidence.
5. Outcome: client work stops falling through the cracks and teams get time back.

## Claim Discipline

Remi copy must be careful about execution claims.

Safe verbs:

- Prepares.
- Drafts.
- Proposes.
- Surfaces.
- Flags.
- Summarizes.
- Tracks.
- Links.
- Routes.
- Assembles.
- Reviews.
- Reminds.
- Helps.

Use only with runtime or receipt evidence:

- Sent.
- Delivered.
- Uploaded.
- Approved.
- Completed.
- Updated.
- Paid.
- Filed.
- Closed.

Avoid claims that imply unbounded autonomy:

- "Remi runs your business for you."
- "Remi replaces your operations team."
- "Remi handles everything automatically."
- "Remi talks to clients without you."
- "Remi completes work whether or not your team reviews it."

### Use Carefully

These words are allowed, but they need context:

- Automate: specify what runs and whether review is required.
- Agent: pair with operations, client work, or follow-through.
- Memory: distinguish conversational memory from durable org intelligence.
- Integration: name the role or surface, not just the logo.
- Execute: pair with approvals, governed tools, or receipts.

| --- | --- |

| Magic | Clear context, governed tools, and receipts |

## Writing Rules

- Lead with the operational outcome, not the AI mechanism.
- Name the work. "Draft the client follow-up" is stronger than "save time."
- Keep sentences clean and direct.
- Use active voice.
- Prefer concrete examples over abstract benefits.
- Use "Remi" as the subject when describing product behavior.
- Keep paragraphs short, especially in web copy.
- Make trust visible when the action is sensitive.
- Avoid exclamation points in product and trust copy.
- Do not use statistics, testimonials, customer names, security claims, or integration counts unless the repo or product source confirms them.

## Stress Contexts

Remi's voice should hold up when the customer is under pressure. Do not apply the same tone intensity everywhere.

### Angry Or Frustrated Customers

Drop any wit. Keep directness, ownership, and next steps.

Use:

"I understand this is frustrating. The draft response is ready for review, with the project note and latest tracker update attached as sources."

Avoid:

"Looks like this slipped through the cracks. Let's clean it up."

### Pricing Or Value Objections

Stay practical. Tie value to recovered time, fewer missed follow-ups, clearer owners, and better client visibility.

Use:

"Remi is for teams where missed follow-ups, unclear ownership, and after-hours admin already cost real time. The demo should make that tradeoff visible."

Avoid:

"Remi pays for itself by transforming every workflow."

### Outages, Incidents, Or Broken Workflows

Be sober, specific, and time-bound. Do not be charming.

Use:

"Some automations are delayed. Existing drafts and approvals are still visible. We are checking affected workflows and will update this page when retries resume."

Avoid:

"Things are a little bumpy right now."

### User Onboarding Confusion

Stay brief and orienting. Tell the user what Remi needs and why.

Use:

"Connect Slack first so Remi can see where client follow-ups already happen."

Avoid:

"Let's unlock the full power of connected intelligence."

### Client-Visible Shared Channels

Be conservative. Remi should not sound casual, chatty, or overfamiliar in front of clients.

Use:

"I can draft a response for your team to review."

Avoid:

"I can jump in and handle the client."

## Surface-Specific Tone

### Homepage And Landing Pages

Be clear, memorable, and outcome-led. Use the strongest point of view here.

Good patterns:

- "The last operations hire you'll ever need."
- "AI tools answer questions. Operations need follow-through."
- "Remi keeps client work from falling through the cracks."

Keep the homepage specific enough to feel like Remi is for client-facing teams, not all knowledge workers everywhere.

### Feature And Product Sections

Be concrete and explanatory. Show what Remi sees, what it connects, and what it prepares.

Good pattern:

"Remi reads the Slack thread, checks the tracker, finds the owner, and drafts the client-safe update for review."

### Trust, Security, And Permissions

Be conservative, precise, and calm. Avoid charm. Explain boundaries.

Good pattern:

"Remi only uses the context a person or workflow is allowed to access."

### In-Product UI

Be brief and useful. The product should not over-explain itself.

Good patterns:

- "Ready to review."
- "Waiting on approval."
- "Source attached."
- "Draft prepared."
- "No owner found."

### Client-Facing Drafts

Be professional, clear, and non-defensive. Do not expose internal uncertainty unless the user asks.

Good pattern:

"Thanks for the note. We are checking the latest project status and will send a clearer update by Friday."

### Blog And Thought Leadership

Be more opinionated than the product UI. Use operational observations, examples, and practical arguments.

Good patterns:

- "Client work breaks in the gaps between tools."
- "The real problem is not another missed task. It is the missing source of truth behind it."

### CTAs

Use plain action verbs and tell the reader what happens next.

Preferred CTA patterns:

- "Get Started."
- "Book a Demo."
- "See Remi in Action."
- "Review the Draft."
- "Approve and Send."
- "Create the Brief."

Avoid vague CTAs when a more specific action is available:

- "Submit."
- "Click Here."
- "Unlock Now."
- "Transform My Workflow."

## Process Checkpoints

Make tone a filter in the workflow, not a reference doc people forget.

Ask these questions before publishing:

- Does this sound like Remi explaining the next step to a busy client-service operator at the end of the day?
- Can a new writer tell where the fence is: what Remi is and what Remi is not?
- Does the copy include at least one concrete task, surface, source, owner, or next step?
- Does it still sound like Remi in a high-pressure context, or does the voice crack into hype, charm, or vagueness?
- Would this line be memorable if every competitor also claimed to be "friendly, expert, and AI-powered"?

Track whether the voice is working where possible:

- Demo conversion from landing pages.
- CTA click-through.
- Reply rates on outbound or lifecycle emails.
- Support resolution time.
- User sentiment in onboarding, support, and cancellation moments.
- Qualitative sales feedback about whether prospects understand what Remi does.

## Before And After Examples

### Generic AI Copy

Avoid:

"Remi uses advanced AI to streamline your workflows and maximize productivity."

Use:

"Remi turns scattered client asks into owners, next steps, and drafts your team can review."

### Overclaiming

Avoid:

"Remi sends follow-ups, updates your CRM, and closes the loop automatically."

Use:

"Remi prepares the follow-up, proposes the CRM update, and shows the sources before your team approves the next step."

### Too Cute

Avoid:

"Say goodbye to chaos and hello to your new AI bestie."

Use:

"When client work gets scattered across Slack, email, and docs, Remi keeps the next step visible."

### Too Abstract

Avoid:

"Bring intelligence to every business process."

Use:

"Prep the meeting brief, draft the client reply, flag the blocker, and keep the owner attached."

## LLM Checklist

Before publishing Remi copy, check:

- Does it match the product facts in `docs/remi-guidelines.md`?
- Does it frame Remi as an operating teammate, not a generic chatbot?
- Does it name a real operational surface or task?
- Does it explain what Remi prepares, proposes, tracks, or executes?
- Does any execution claim need approval, receipt, or ledger evidence?
- Does it sound useful before it sounds clever?
- Would a busy agency, consultancy, or service-business operator recognize the problem?
- Is the copy specific enough to stand apart from generic AI SaaS language?
- Does it avoid generic adjective soup?
- Does it show contrast: what Remi is and what Remi is not?
- Has the tone been adjusted for pressure, risk, or client visibility?
- Could a new writer use this as an example of Remi's voice?

## Maintenance

Update this guide when:

- Remi's product positioning changes in `docs/remi-guidelines.md`.
- The product gains or removes a meaningful capability.
- Customer research reveals better audience language.
- A new surface needs a distinct tone pattern.
- Existing copy starts drifting toward generic AI or vague SaaS language.
- A high-pressure scenario exposes a gap in the tone rules.
