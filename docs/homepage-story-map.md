# Homepage Story Map

Date: 2026-07-09
Status: working story architecture, agreed in discussion with Hunter. This doc defines the narrative: what each chapter must accomplish, in what order, and the tests it must pass. It deliberately contains no drafted copy; wordsmithing is Hunter's. Competitive rationale and the section-by-section defect list live in `docs/site-copy-audit-2026-07-09.md`. Positioning canon lives in the remi repo (`docs/product/positioning.md`, `PRODUCT.md`, `docs/product/hero-persona.md`).

## The reader

Mike Reeves: owner-operator remodeler/GC, $300k-$1.5M, no office manager. On a jobsite by 7, phone ringing unanswered because he's holding a level, quotes typed in the truck at lunch, invoices at the kitchen table at 9pm. Five figures uncollected right now and he hates being the bad guy about it. Quotes die because he answered six hours late. Disputes come down to his word against the client's memory. Runs the whole operation from an iPhone, fluently. Not anti-technology; anti-software. Distrusts "AI" as a sales word the way he distrusts extended warranties. Hires people, not products: he knows how to onboard a helper, check their work, and give them more rope.

The counterfactual in his head is not another app. It is the office manager he has needed for years and cannot justify hiring.

## The story in one breath

You hire Remi. She gets you paid and runs the business side; you run the work the business actually does. She earns trust like any new hire: small jobs first, everything on the record, nothing goes out without your yes. And because she's always watching, she texts you first. Simple enough that you run your business from your text messages.

The narrative container is **meeting a new hire**. Every chapter is a stage of that meeting: she knows your problem, watch her work, how she operates her first week, the job split, who vouches for her, the terms of hire, and a small reversible first job. This container matters because the reader has never evaluated software successfully, but he has hired dozens of people and knows exactly how to judge one.

## Register rules (summary; canon wins on detail)

- Every line passes the Facebook-group test: would it survive being read aloud in a contractors' Facebook group.
- Pain order: 1) money uncollected, 2) quotes dying from silence, 3) nothing written down, 4) fear of handing over the keys, 5) admin hours last, never the headline.
- Pains are named in money and people, never in work categories. He does not have "an admin problem"; Henderson hasn't paid him.
- Claim discipline: prepares, drafts, flags, watches freely; sent, paid, done only with approval or receipt framing. No "does it all," no "so simple," no superlatives he has heard from every salesman before.
- "AI" never leads. The disclosure appears once, low, at the close, as straight talk.
- No em dashes. No invented numbers anywhere; the sourced stats bank carries problem proof until cohort receipts exist.

## The chapters

### Chapter 0: Hero (the thesis)

Job: state the belief and qualify the reader.

- H1 stays: "Your business should run. It shouldn't run you." Ratified, not up for revision.
- The subhead's job: say who she is for and what she does, in owner words, with zero AI vocabulary. It must self-qualify the reader the way the ad doctrine demands: an owner leans in, everyone else bounces. The division-of-labor idea (she runs the business side, you run the work) is the strongest candidate frame and is already canon phrasing.
- One true availability statement, consistent with the announcement bar and the blog.
- Two live CTAs. "Text Remi" is the differentiated one and must be real before it renders; a texting-native reader who taps a dead texting button is gone.

### Chapter 1: Recognition ("Meet Remi")

Job: the "oh shit, this is me" moment. He sees his own week on the page.

- "Meet Remi" stays as the beat opener; it is how you introduce a person, and it starts the hire narrative for free.
- The payoff line must move from mood to scene: named, concrete moments involving money and people (the invoice he's embarrassed to chase twice, the quote that died from a slow reply, the thing he told the client that nobody wrote down). "Work that follows you home" is the setting for these scenes, not the pitch.
- Test: a SaaS founder or agency owner reading the line should NOT think "that's me too." If the line is universal, it is not qualifying.
- The rotating task chips already carry half the recognition; keep the device, keep them money-weighted.
- Image alt texts get the same ICP discipline as visible copy.

### Chapter 2: Watch her work (iPhone scenarios)

Job: proof by demonstration. The interview where the candidate shows her work.

- This is the strongest section on the current site. The scenario set and its pain-order mapping (Get paid first) stay.
- Structural fix: make "she texted first" impossible to miss. In the "Get paid" thread Remi initiates unprompted, which is the single claim no competitor can make, and a skimming reader will not notice who sent the first message. The framing around the phone must carry it.
- Emoji density pass; the iMessage fiction earns some, not this many.
- Every thread keeps the approval mechanics visible ("Prepared. I will wait for your approval"): this plants the seed the close will harvest.

### Chapter 3: Her first week (how it works)

Job: mechanism, told as onboarding a hire rather than describing a system.

- Three beats, sequenced as time: connect the inbox; she learns how your business actually runs; then she starts speaking up first.
- The morning brief should appear here as the concrete recurring artifact he can picture.
- "You approve the next move" lands last and hard; it is the hinge between capability and trust, and it sets up the close.

### Chapter 4: The job split (You / Remi)

Job: make the division of labor legible in one glance. This replaces the "usual way vs with Remi" comparison table.

- Reframe from before/after comparison to **her job description**: what you keep (the craft, the clients, the decisions) and what she takes (the chasing, the paperwork, the remembering). A job description is the one document every owner knows how to read.
- The money row exists and leads; the number-one pain cannot be absent from the section that enumerates the split.
- "Run your business from your phone" is not this section's header. It is the capstone payoff line and moves to the top of the close.

### Chapter 5: References (real-world proof)

Job: who vouches for her. Placement after mechanism, before the close, is correct.

- Content is gated on real cohort receipts; the current case-study files are design stubs and stay untouched per Hunter.
- Design the slot now for the target format: named person, real shop, one number (challenge, what she did, receipts, zero sends without approval).
- Interim: the sourced stats bank carries problem proof honestly ($17.5k average unpaid invoices, 47% of invoices 30+ days overdue, 78% hire the first responder). True today, no customers required.

### Chapter 6: The close (terms of hire)

Job: make it safe to say yes. Not a comparison, not a feature recap; the end of a hiring conversation. Opens with the capstone line ("run your business from your phone" territory) as the after-picture, then answers the four closing objections in his order:

1. **What happens when she screws up?** Concede fallibility plainly: capable, not infallible, and nothing goes out without your yes. Nobody in the market answers this question at his register; the approval architecture makes it cheap and true for Remi. Two honest sentences here outweigh any capability claim on the page.
2. **What am I risking?** Reversibility: every action carries a receipt, ask where a number came from and she shows you, and if you fire her you keep everything (export). This is also where the AI disclosure line lives; at the decision moment, "she's AI and we'll never pretend otherwise" reads as the straight talk that separates Remi from everyone who tried to sneak the word past him.
3. **What does it cost?** The ratified ladder (Pro $99, Scale $199), plainly, no credit math anywhere near it. The office-manager anchor is available and is the one comparison that belongs at the close, because it is a hiring comparison: the hire he's needed for years at a price he can finally justify. (Candidate frame to test, not a decision.)
4. **What's the first step, and how small is it?** The audit frame closes the story: let her look at your inbox, you get the report of what's slipping, we delete everything unless you hire her. Bounded lookback and the deletion promise stated as the offer, not fine print. This is a free estimate, the exact play he runs on his own customers; he has closed a thousand deals with it from the other side.

### Chapter 7: The human

Job: a name and a face. Skeptics hire people, not companies.

- Small section near the close: who built her, why, one honest paragraph. Chicago is an asset here.
- This substitutes for the testimonials he does not believe yet and outperforms them for this segment.

### Chapter 8: FAQ (objection cleanup)

Job: answer his actual questions in his voice. The eight-question owner-voiced set: what can she do without me; will she contact my customers without asking; how does she learn my business; do I have to use iMessage; is my data safe and what happens to it if I quit; what does it cost and what happens in the trial; how long does setup take; what happens when she's wrong. The last one is open ground nobody in the market touches.

## Story-level tests

Run the finished page against these before shipping:

- Does the story read as meeting a hire from first line to close, without ever calling her "software," "a tool," "an assistant," or "an agent"?
- Does money appear before minutes? (Pain order check on every section in sequence.)
- Is "she speaks first" both demonstrated (chapter 2) and named (chapter 3)?
- Does every capability moment have its approval shadow visible?
- Could every number on the page survive a source request?
- Does the page ever ask for belief before it has shown work? (Chapters 1-2 show; claims come after.)
- Is there exactly one conversion ask, repeated, and does it read as the free estimate?
- Would the whole page survive being read aloud in a contractors' Facebook group?
