# Late Payments Cost the Average Small Business About $39,000 a Year. Here's the Math.

Late payments cost the average small business about **$39,000 a year**. That figure comes from a 2025 survey by Gateway Commercial Finance, in which owners estimated losing an average of $39,406 annually to payment delays, [reported by Inc.](https://www.inc.com/bruce-crumley/unpaid-customer-invoices-are-piling-up-squeezing-small-businesses/91198319). It is not one bill you can point to. It is five smaller costs stacked on top of each other: the interest on money you float while you wait, the hours you spend chasing, the invoices you eventually write off, the months you pay yourself late, and the work you turn down because your cash is stuck in someone else's pocket.

This post builds that number from parts, then hands you a worksheet to compute your own. The number is real because the parts are real, and if you are owed money right now, you are already paying most of them. Remi is an AI teammate that watches your books and flags invoices before they go stale, but you do not need to hire anyone to run the math below. You just need ten minutes and last year's numbers.

## What does $39,000 a year in late payments actually come from?

It comes from five buckets, not one. Owners feel late payments as a vague cash squeeze, so the true cost hides. Break it into pieces and it stops hiding. Here is an illustrative example for a service business doing about $400,000 a year that is typically owed around $30,000 at once. These are example numbers to show the shape of the cost, not a survey result.

| Cost bucket | What it is | Illustrative annual cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Financing gap | Interest on the money you float while invoices sit unpaid | $4,500 |
| Chase time | Hours you spend following up, valued at your hourly rate | $9,400 |
| Write-offs | Invoices you never collect and eventually write off | $8,000 |
| Delayed owner pay | Fees and interest from covering gaps with personal money | $3,000 |
| Opportunity cost | Margin on the one job you passed on for lack of cash | $14,000 |
| **Total** | | **~$38,900** |

That lands right around the $39,000 survey figure without straining. The two lines most owners never count are the first and the last: the cost of money and the cost of the work you did not take. We will come back to both.

The scale of the problem is not in doubt. QuickBooks' 2025 Late Payments Report found the average small business is owed about **$17,500 in unpaid invoices**, and that **47% of businesses have invoices more than 30 days past due**, [per Intuit QuickBooks](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/). In the trades it is worse. Built's 2025 research found roughly **two out of three contractors are paid late**, with 70% reporting regular payment delays, [reported by Built](https://getbuilt.com/blog/70-percent-of-contractors-say-payment-delays-threaten-industry/). Getting paid on time is now the exception, not the norm.

## How much are late payments really costing you?

The average is a starting point, not your number. Your number depends on how much you are owed, what you pay to borrow, and how much work you turn away. Run this worksheet with last year's numbers. Every step is one line of arithmetic.

1. **Financing gap.** Estimate the average amount you are owed at any one time. Multiply it by your real cost of borrowing, whichever card, line, or factoring rate you actually use to bridge the gap. Floating $20,000 on a 15% line is $3,000 a year. This is money you pay for the privilege of being owed.
2. **Chase time.** Count the hours a week you or someone in the business spends following up on invoices. Multiply by the weeks you work, then by what an hour of your time is worth. Two hours a week at $60 across 48 weeks is $5,760.
3. **Write-offs.** Add up the invoices from last year you never collected. If you do not track it, use 1% to 3% of revenue as a placeholder until you do. On $400,000 that is $4,000 to $12,000.
4. **Delayed owner pay.** For any month you paid yourself late or dipped into personal savings to cover the business, add the fees, overdraft charges, or credit interest that caused. This is small on paper and heavy in real life. Nearly **1 in 3 owners has delayed paying themselves** because of late payments, [per Bluevine](https://www.bluevine.com/blog/small-business-late-payment-gaps).
5. **Opportunity cost.** Name one job, hire, or purchase you passed on last year because your cash was tied up in unpaid invoices. Write down the margin you would have made. In the same Gateway survey, **63% of owners said a late payment caused them to miss a growth opportunity**, [reported by Inc.](https://www.inc.com/bruce-crumley/unpaid-customer-invoices-are-piling-up-squeezing-small-businesses/91198319).

Add the five numbers. That total is what getting paid late costs you in a year. Most owners who run this the first time land higher than they expected, because steps one and five never show up on a bank statement.

## Why is the financing gap the most expensive part?

Because you pay it every single day the invoice is open, and you pay it in interest, not in effort. When a customer owes you $17,500 and you cover payroll with a credit card at 22%, that unpaid invoice is quietly costing you real money while it sits there. The Gateway survey found the average owner reports late payments eating **11% of annual revenue**, and nearly 70% said they leaned on personal savings or took on debt to cover the gap, [reported by Inc.](https://www.inc.com/bruce-crumley/unpaid-customer-invoices-are-piling-up-squeezing-small-businesses/91198319).

The fix is not more borrowing. It is shortening the gap: get invoices out the day the work is done, and follow up early and consistently so nothing drifts to 60 or 90 days. Both are boring. Both work. The next section is the manual playbook.

## How do you actually shrink the number without hiring anyone?

You shorten the time between finishing the work and the money landing. Here is the full method, usable tonight with nothing but your invoicing tool and a calendar.

1. **Invoice the day you finish, not the end of the month.** Every day an invoice waits to be sent is a day added to the clock. Send it same-day, with clear terms and a due date on the invoice itself.
2. **Put your terms in writing before the work starts.** Due date, accepted payment methods, deposit, and any late fee. A late fee you never mentioned is not a late fee, it is an argument.
3. **Set three follow-up touches per invoice.** A friendly nudge two days before the due date, a firmer one the day after, and a direct one at seven days late. Most invoices get paid on touch two or three. Silence is the enemy.
4. **Make it trivial to pay.** A payment link in the invoice beats "mail a check" every time. Remove one step and you remove one excuse.
5. **Escalate on a schedule, not a mood.** At 30 days, a phone call. At 60, a formal notice. Decide the ladder once, in calm, so you are not deciding it angry at 9pm.

Here is a follow-up sequence you can copy. Fill in the brackets and send verbatim.

> Two days before due: "Hi [Name], quick heads up that invoice [#] for [$amount] is due on [date]. Here is the link to pay: [link]. Thanks again for the work, it was good working with you."
>
> Day after due: "Hi [Name], invoice [#] for [$amount] was due yesterday and I do not see it landed yet. If it is already on the way, ignore this. If not, here is the link: [link]. Appreciate you."
>
> Seven days late: "Hi [Name], invoice [#] for [$amount] is now a week past due. I want to keep things clear between us, so can you let me know the date I should expect payment? Link is here if that is easiest: [link]."
> - Follow-up sequence you can send as-is

On late fees: charging one is often allowed if the fee is stated up front and stays reasonable, but the rules **vary by state**, so check your state's rules before you set a policy. QuickBooks keeps a plain-language [guide to late fees and interest on invoices](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/payments/late-payment-fees/). This is general information, not legal advice.

## What is the cheapest change with the biggest payoff?

Sending the invoice faster and following up on a fixed schedule. It costs nothing and attacks the two most expensive buckets at once: the financing gap shrinks because the clock starts and ends sooner, and write-offs shrink because an invoice you chase at day one rarely becomes the invoice you abandon at day ninety.

The catch is consistency. The method works only if you do it on every invoice, every week, when you are tired and the phone is ringing. That is exactly where it breaks down for most owners, and where the cost quietly rebuilds itself.

### How do I calculate what late payments cost my business?

Add five numbers: your financing gap (average amount owed at once times your borrowing rate), your chase hours valued at your hourly rate, last year's write-offs, the fees or interest from any month you covered a gap with personal money, and the margin on one job you passed on for lack of cash.
