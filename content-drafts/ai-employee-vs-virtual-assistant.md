# AI Employee vs Virtual Assistant: Which Should an Owner-Run Business Hire?

A virtual assistant is a person you hire by the hour to do tasks you hand them. An AI teammate like Remi is software that watches your connected tools and speaks up first. For an owner-run business, the honest split is this: hire a VA for phone calls, in-person errands, and judgment-heavy work, and use an AI teammate for the always-on watching, the follow-ups, and the records. Plenty of owners end up using both.

The cost gap is real but it hides the real question. A US-based virtual assistant averages about $25.75 an hour, per [Indeed's salary data](https://www.indeed.com/career/virtual-assistant/salaries). An AI teammate runs a flat monthly fee. But money is not the deciding factor, because the two do not do the same job. A VA is a pair of hands you direct. An AI teammate is a set of eyes that never blinks. This guide gives you an honest side-by-side, the cases where each one clearly wins, and a way to decide without guessing.

## What is the difference between an AI employee and a virtual assistant?

A virtual assistant is a remote human contractor. An AI employee, or more honestly an AI teammate, is software. That single fact drives every other difference.

A VA waits for instructions. You hand off a task, they do it, they come back. They can call a customer, sit on hold with a supplier, run an errand near your office, and read a tense email thread and tell you it feels off. They are a person, so they bring judgment, a voice, and hands.

An AI teammate works the other way around. Instead of waiting for a task, it watches the sources you connect, your inbox and your accounting tool, and it raises its hand when something is slipping. It does not sleep, take a lunch break, or forget the invoice from three weeks ago. But it lives inside your tools. It reads, tracks, and drafts. It does not pick up the phone or show up in person.

Remi is an AI teammate. We say so plainly because she will never pretend otherwise, and neither will we.

## How much does each one cost?

Here is the honest money picture. A US VA is the most expensive option per hour, an offshore VA is cheaper, and an AI teammate is a flat fee that does not scale with hours.

| Option | Typical cost | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| US-based virtual assistant | ~$25.75/hour average, $30 to $75/hour for experienced ([Indeed](https://www.indeed.com/career/virtual-assistant/salaries)) | Hours you direct; you pay for time whether it produces or not |
| Offshore virtual assistant | ~$3 to $10/hour, roughly $800 to $2,400/month full-time ([Wishup](https://www.wishup.co/blog/how-much-does-a-virtual-assistant-cost/), [Hire Overseas](https://www.hireoverseas.com/blogs/virtual-assistant-cost)) | Hours you direct, at a lower rate, usually with a time-zone gap |
| AI teammate (Remi) | $99/month Pro, $199/month Scale | Always-on watching and drafting; you approve the actions |

The trap is comparing the numbers alone. Ten hours a week of a US VA is about $1,000 a month and buys you exactly ten hours. A flat monthly AI fee buys you eyes on your books every hour of every day, but zero phone calls. You are not choosing the cheaper line. You are choosing what job you need done.

## The honest side-by-side

Neither one is better across the board. Each wins on different rows.

| What matters | Virtual assistant | AI teammate (Remi) |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | $3 to $75+/hour, scales with hours used | Flat $99 to $199/month, does not scale with volume |
| Hours and availability | Set schedule, time off, one time zone | Always on, no breaks, watches continuously |
| Training burden | Real onboarding, documented processes, weeks of correction | Connect your sources; it reads and starts flagging |
| Consistency | Varies by person, mood, and turnover | Same process every time, does not forget or drift |
| Judgment | Human read on nuance, tone, and edge cases | Pattern-based; flags what it sees, you make the call |
| Trust and verification | You trust the person; verifying their work takes effort | Every action carries a receipt and a source you can check |

Read the table honestly. A person beats software on judgment and voice. Software beats a person on always-on watching, consistency, and a verifiable record. Turnover is a hidden VA cost: when a good one leaves, their knowledge of your business walks out too. An AI teammate does not quit, but it also cannot smooth over an awkward phone call with a slow-paying customer.

## When should you hire a virtual assistant instead?

Hire a person when the work needs a voice, hands, or real-time human judgment. Software cannot do these well, and pretending otherwise wastes your money.

A VA is the right call when:

- The work is phone-heavy. Answering calls, calling customers back, sitting on hold with a supplier, or booking appointments by voice.
- The task is physical or local. Picking something up, dropping something off, being somewhere in person.
- The judgment is genuinely complex. A delicate client relationship, a negotiation, a situation where the wrong tone loses the job.
- You need someone to own a whole process end to end, adapt on the fly, and use discretion you would trust a new hire to use.

If your bottleneck is that nobody is answering the phone during a job, no amount of software fixes that. You need a person.

## When is an AI teammate the better hire?

Choose an AI teammate when the job is watching, remembering, and drafting, and when consistency matters more than a human touch. This is exactly the work owners drop first when they get busy.

An AI teammate wins when:

- Something needs constant watching. Invoices going stale, quotes that got no reply, a customer waiting on an answer. Small businesses are owed about $17,500 in unpaid invoices on average, and 47% of those invoices are already 30-plus days past due, per the [2025 QuickBooks Small Business Late Payments Report](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/r/small-business-data/small-business-late-payments-report-2025/). A person watching for that around the clock is expensive. Software is not.
- Follow-ups keep falling through. Speed matters: responding to a lead in 5 minutes rather than 30 makes you about 21 times more likely to qualify it, per the [MIT and InsideSales Lead Response Management study](https://www.leadresponsemanagement.org/lrm_study). An always-on teammate flags the reply that is overdue.
- Everything needs to be on the record. Receipts, sources, an exportable history, so nothing lives only in your head.
- You want drafts, not decisions. Remi drafts the payment reminder or the quote follow-up and you approve it before it goes out. Nothing sends without your say-so.

That last point is the guardrail. Remi prepares, drafts, flags, tracks, and reminds. Things get sent or marked paid only with your approval, and every action carries a receipt you can check.

## Can you use both?

Yes, and for a lot of owners that is the honest answer. The two tools cover different gaps, so pairing them is not overkill. It is the clean division of labor.

The split that works: let the AI teammate own the watching, drafting, and record-keeping layer. It monitors the books, flags what is going stale, and drafts the follow-up for your approval, every hour of every day. Then use a VA for the human layer, the phone calls, the in-person errands, and the judgment calls that need a real voice. The AI teammate can even tee up the work, flagging the three customers who need a call, so your VA's hours go to the calls instead of the digging.

You do not have to pick a side. You have to match each job to the thing that does it best.

## How do you decide which to hire first?

Decide by naming your single worst bottleneck, then hiring the tool built for it. Do not buy a category. Buy a fix.

1. Write down the one task that is actually costing you money or sleep right now. Be specific. "Invoices going unpaid" is different from "phone rings during jobs and nobody answers."
2. Classify it. Does it need a voice or hands (a person), or does it need constant watching, drafting, and records (an AI teammate)?
3. Check the frequency. A once-a-week batch of calls points to part-time human help. A thing that needs eyes on it every single day points to software.
4. Match the budget to the job, not the other way around. Do not hire a $1,000-a-month VA to do watching that a flat fee handles, and do not ask software to make the calls a person should make.
5. Start with the one bottleneck. Add the other tool when a second, clearly different gap shows up.

## FAQ

### Is an AI teammate cheaper than a virtual assistant?

Usually, yes, on paper. A US virtual assistant averages about $25.75 an hour, per [Indeed](https://www.indeed.com/career/virtual-assistant/salaries), and even a full-time offshore VA runs roughly $800 to $2,400 a month. An AI teammate like Remi is a flat $99 to $199 a month. But they do different jobs, so the cheaper number is not automatically the right hire.

### Can an AI teammate make phone calls for me?

Remi does not place phone calls. She works over text, email, Slack, and a web portal. If your bottleneck is calling customers, answering the phone, or chasing people by voice, that is still a job for a person.

### Do I have to train an AI teammate like I train a virtual assistant?

Less, and differently. A VA needs onboarding, documented processes, and correction over weeks. An AI teammate reads the sources you connect and starts flagging what it sees. You still steer it, but you are approving drafts, not writing a training manual.

### Can I use a virtual assistant and an AI teammate at the same time?

Yes, and many owners do. The clean split is an AI teammate for the always-on watching, drafting, and record-keeping, and a VA for phone calls, in-person errands, and judgment-heavy work. They cover different gaps.

### What happens to my data if I stop using an AI teammate?

With Remi, everything she tracked stays yours. The receipts, sources, and history are exportable, so firing the software does not erase the record of what happened.
